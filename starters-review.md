## 2. Hiring manager *(sourced from the bank's most-asked questions)*

**1. A disagreement with engineering, and how she resolved it** *[Bank Q18 and Q22, the most frequent behavioural question in the bank: Socure, Workato, Treasure Data, Amazon]*
> The clearest example is the caching decision on Intuit Assist. Adding the fifty most common inline points of need as conversation starters caused a sharp rise in calls to the model; some of those points were hit five to seven million times. Engineering flagged the cost, but proposed no mitigation. She worked with the engineering manager to quantify the cost against the volume, chose a scrappy short-term cache, held per session for roughly three days, in preference to a long build, and split the team, so that one half worked on caching while the other passed screen context to the model. Costs fell by about 27%, and she set the full caching strategy as a fast follow across TurboTax, QuickBooks and Mailchimp.

**2. How does she prioritize when everything is a priority?** *[Bank Q45 and Q21: Treasure Data, Asana, Socure, ServiceTitan]*
> She anchors on the goal and on the customer problem, and then weighs the cost of not solving it, which is very often direct revenue. From there she stack-ranks against the other priorities, cuts scope where she can, keeps the backlog disciplined, and, where she must escalate, does so through a documented template agreed with the engineering manager.

**3. How did she know the generative AI product was ready to ship?** *[Bank Q70, Q82, Q92: Workato, Gusto, Netflix]*
> Quality came first, because in a generative product the response is the product. She set a baseline of 80% accuracy and a bar of 90%, and she refused to ship below it, even two weeks from launch and with engineers pressing to release. She then found the root cause: the intent taxonomy needed sub-classification, distinguishing a single W-2 from several, and from none at all. Accuracy rose from 80% to 94% in a single iteration. A delay, in her view, is acceptable; a poor answer is not.

**4. Build or buy, and how does she manage the cost of large language models?** *[Bank Q50 and Q43: Spotify, Socure, Gigamon]*
> On building or buying, she weighs the company's goals, the stack it already has, the time required to reach maturity, the cost, and the fit on integration and on security, usually through a formal RFP. The chat interface for Intuit Assist, for instance, was bought from a third party and then wrapped in Intuit's own content and branding. On cost, she classifies questions by volume, caches those asked most often, and pre-generates the answers to known product questions, so that the model need not be called at all.

## 6. Engineer or data scientist *(sourced from her real technical rounds)*

**1. How does she approach RAG, and how did she evaluate its accuracy?** *[Bank Q105: NVIDIA]*
> At Intuit the retrieval was deliberately simple, drawing only upon Intuit's own content, which required a good deal of legal and compliance work to untangle. She built a gold set of roughly 2,500 questions, and scored five metrics against it: accuracy, coherence, hallucination, disambiguation and completeness, with tone and redaction treated as table stakes. Experts evaluated the answers by hand every fortnight. The product launched only once each metric stood at 90% or above.

**2. How did she raise accuracy from 80% to more than 90%?** *[Bank Q106: NVIDIA]*
> By re-clustering at the level of the sub-topic: a W-2 became a single W-2, several W-2s, or none at all. The metrics moved sharply as a result. Completeness improved after the first release, once the content of the IRS and Intuit's own expert publications had been added.

**3. Which models and retrievers did she use?** *[Bank Q107: NVIDIA]*
> GPT-3.5, GPT-4, GPT-4o-mini and Haiku, alongside the data team's own models. The retriever was a simple RAG implementation at the time; the direction of travel is towards graph-based retrieval.

**4. How does she catch hallucinations in production?** *[Bank Q79: Walmart]*
> With hierarchical, rule-based alerts, at the level of topic, sub-topic and sub-sub-topic, together with alerts for a failed model, for a failed system, and for uptime and latency. The common triggers were untrained variables, such as state taxes, single-phrase or ambiguous utterances, and the placement of keywords. Where there was no content at all, the system fell back gracefully. Nightly dumps and morning dashboards did the rest.
